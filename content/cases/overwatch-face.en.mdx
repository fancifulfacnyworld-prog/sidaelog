Overwatch wasn't the first game to build a hero-centered FPS.<br/>
And yet, for many people, it became the face of the hero-centered FPS.
<br/>
We usually think first of new heroes, new abilities, new team play.<br/>
But what made Overwatch special lies less in what it added than in what it gave up.<br/>
Back then, the FPS market leaned heavily on a military sensibility.<br/>
Realistic firearms, lifelike battlefields, an atmosphere that evoked actual war.<br/>
Many games were competing in the same direction.
<br/>
Overwatch didn't follow that current.<br/>
It set down realism and the gun-centered kind of fun.<br/>
More than how well you shoot or how many headshots you land, what mattered became which abilities a character has.<br/>
It moved the center of the FPS from the gun to the character.<br/>
Of course, class-based team FPS wasn't a new concept in itself.
<br/>
But Overwatch added character narrative and worldbuilding on top of that structure, changing the very way you approach an FPS.<br/>
Where the military sensibility was given up, personality moved in.<br/>
Heroes carrying the cultures and characters of places around the world filled that space.<br/>
Players weren't choosing a gun — they were choosing a hero they loved.
<br/>
Where the realistic battlefield was given up, the game's world moved in.<br/>
Over the fun of racking up kills alone, it chose the fun of a team completing its roles together.<br/>
All of these choices ran a little against the FPS trend of the time.
<br/>
But because of that,<br/>
Overwatch became not one of countless shooters, but a new face.<br/>
Anyone can follow a trend.<br/>
But to make something feel new, you may have to give something up.<br/>
That's why a game's choices leave behind a philosophy all its own.
<br/>
What Overwatch threw away wasn't the gun or the realism,<br/>
but the fixed belief that "an FPS must be a certain way."<br/>
That choice<br/>
made Overwatch an IP that defines its era.
