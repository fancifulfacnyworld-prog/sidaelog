Why did I do it?<br/>
Maybe because it felt closer to the right answer.<br/>
Maybe because I assumed a right answer existed at all.<br/>
<br/>
From the very start, the chatter between the protagonist's neurons drew me in naturally, and without realizing it I locked myself into a role — as one of his neurons too.<br/>
<br/>
The most absurd choice came when the hotel manager said I had to pay back the money.<br/>
I'd already tried paying once, so this time I decided to skip out without paying.<br/>
The character suddenly flung his whole body into some flailing stunt and died in a freak accident. All I'd wanted was to keep my money to buy a book from the shop.<br/>
<br/>
Even in the moments I chose for myself — wasn't I being nudged toward a particular option?
<br/>
There are so many choices that it looks free, like I can pick whatever I want.<br/>
Throughout the dialogue the character's inner selves chime in — one feeding you knowledge, one provoking you — seeming to offer information from many angles.<br/>
But in practice, they cut into your judgment so you never get a quiet moment to weigh things alone.<br/>
In the end, you choose in the direction the voices have been pulling.
<br/>
A normal UI delivers information to the user.<br/>
It points the way and helps you play.<br/>
But Disco Elysium's UI gives you nothing. It renders the thoughts happening inside the character's head as if they were UI, keeping you thinking only within that head.
<br/>
It isn't a game that grants freedom of choice — it's a game that confines thought.<br/>
Because what's handed to you as options is the character's own thought process, even when we feel we're choosing for ourselves, we can't choose freely.<br/>
(The questions and answers from my own brain aren't on the menu!)
<br/>
And so we end up interpreting our own choices.
<br/>
This game has no right answer.<br/>
You have to read the options and make sense of them.<br/>
That's why it's designed to keep you lingering in the act of choosing.
<br/>
So why did the game design it this way?
<br/>
What the game wants is a structure that builds an interpretable experience and gets people to share it.<br/>
So it turned the act of interpreting into the thing you play.<br/>
<br/>
If you buy Disco Elysium after watching a playthrough, you can play it easily — but you'll struggle to have a good experience.<br/>
Even making the same choices won't make it the same game.<br/>
Because everyone's reasons for choosing are different.<br/>
When the playthrough ends, players start talking about their choices and what they lived through.
<br/>
Only after the game is over does the "real" thing it designed begin.
