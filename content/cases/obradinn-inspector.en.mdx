The case begins from death.<br/>
If the player had been at the scene from the very start, wouldn't the game feel more urgent,<br/>
more alive?
<br/>
The whole crew of the Obra Dinn is presumed to have died and returned — so why didn't the game<br/>
hand the player the role of a detective?
<br/>
If it began at the very moment the events unfolded, what we'd feel is confusion and overwhelm.<br/>
Should I rescue this character? Should I act alongside them?<br/>
This game didn't want that.<br/>
So it begins from a case already over — from death.
<br/>
The player is naturally placed in the position of an observer.
<br/>
The reason we became the chief inspector is much the same.<br/>
A detective divides good from evil.<br/>
It's a choice that stirs the player's sense of justice. But an inspector?
<br/>
An inspector simply searches for the facts from a third party's vantage and observes the case — with no need to feel anything else.<br/>
Even getting it wrong is read as part of observing, so no penalty was applied.
<br/>
Return of the Obra Dinn seems to strip away,<br/>
as much as it can, the emotions that get in the way of observing.<br/>
The UI, too, melts into the screen and works as if it isn't there.
<br/>
Because of that, you come to feel — naturally — just how crucial observing the scene really is.
