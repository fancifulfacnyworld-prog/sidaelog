Even before launch, Overwatch gathered enormous anticipation on its cinematics alone.<br/>
Most games make fans only after you've felt fun combat, growth systems, and great balance.
<br/>
But Overwatch was a little different.<br/>
It made you love the heroes before the game.
<br/>
The heroes aren't merely playable characters; they're introduced like protagonists.<br/>
The Pixar-like staging felt less like a video introducing a game and more like a film introducing a new world.<br/>
What's interesting is that most of the cinematics don't explain the gameplay at all.
<br/>
Rather than how you shoot or how you use an ultimate,<br/>
they show you first why a hero fights and what kind of person they are.<br/>
It sold the heroes' stories before the game systems.<br/>
So people already knew the heroes before they ever played.<br/>
Someone loved Tracer, someone rooted for Mei, someone was curious about Reaper's story.<br/>
The path to becoming a fan was laid first — without ever touching the game.
<br/>
The first product Overwatch sold wasn't the game.<br/>
It was the anticipation of "I want to step into this world and meet these heroes."<br/>
Play came after.
<br/>
You can ask a similar question when building an IP.<br/>
When does a game begin?<br/>
The moment you press the download button?<br/>
Or the moment you first meet a character?<br/>
Overwatch offers a slightly different answer.
<br/>
A game can begin before the play does.<br/>
The moment a player comes to love a character.<br/>
Overwatch's first play began not when you fire a gun, but the moment you find a hero you love.
