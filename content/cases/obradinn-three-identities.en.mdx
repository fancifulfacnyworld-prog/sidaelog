Around the halfway mark, the game ground to a halt.<br/>

Unable to find the memory that came next, I kept circling the same spot.<br/>

When it still wouldn't give on the second day, another thought struck me.<br/>

Don't I have to identify people to reach the next chapter?<br/>
<br/>
At that point, I was certain of only one person's identity.<br/>

But registering that one person made nothing happen.<br/>
<br/>
Wondering how to crack it, I checked the first task.<br/>

Confirm three identities.<br/>
<br/>
In Return of the Obra Dinn, you don't just match names.<br/>

You have to match nationality and occupation along with them.<br/>
<br/>
It's a tough condition, but you might guess one person right.<br/>

You could pin one vague clue and land it by chance.<br/>

That's why the challenge was never just a single person.<br/>
<br/>
Matching three changes everything.<br/>

You have to follow the flow of events and read the relationships between people.<br/>

The player lingers longer inside the memories.<br/>

You build a hypothesis from what you've observed, and when you're wrong you come back and observe again.<br/>

<PullQuote>

Three, rather than one,<br />

is the number where guessing gives way to cross-verification.

</PullQuote>

So this condition isn't simply a device to crank up the difficulty.<br/>

It's closer to a tutorial.<br/>
<br/>
Without a single kind explanation, it teaches the player how this game is meant to be solved.<br/>
<br/>
Observe, connect, be certain.<br/>

It isn't only to delay revealing the answer.<br/>
It's to make you learn how to be certain of an identity.
